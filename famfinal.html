Our Colored Bodies//Paint on plywood with projection//"The events in Charlottesville, VA shocked the nation in August. There seems to be a sense of complacency when our society talks about race as if this country has made significant progress. There is this idea that being “color blind” puts all races on equal playing fields. However, we cannot ignore that race is part of our histories, the very histories that affect the way we relate to others and to ourselves. So much of our lives are directed by the things we come to terms with – our race, our time in history, and our place of birth. The way we negotiate ourselves in this world is undoubtedly shaped by our experiences. This is why I have portrayed my mother, father and brother in my wall piece. I have been thinking about my experience as a second-generation Korean-American, as a first-generation college student, and as a female. Perhaps it is a privilege to never have to question your place in this nation. Perhaps it is an ignorant bliss to never be frustrated with the racism that persists. It took an appalling and disgusting event to wake up the nation and speak up loudly against racism, but Charlottesville did not come from out of the blue. Racism did not just come back. It’s been around and it must be talked about."
